Chinese brocade is intricate and stunning Here s where to find the real deal TRAVEL Chinese brocade is intricate and stunning Here s where to find the real deal This complex cloth once reserved for the elite is finding new life on fashion runways At the Nanjing Brocade Museum an artisan weaves traditional Chinese brocade on looms that are so intricate they require workers to execute dozens of procedures Photograph by CHINA PHOTOS GETTY IMAGES By Ronan O Connell October min read Flames beasts weapons and stars flow from wooden looms to embellish brocade a year old fabric once reserved for China s elite and now popular with young fashion designers Flowers are a common brocade motif as in this silk fragment from the mid century Photograph from METROPOLITAN MUSEUM OF ART This art form is so complex that even veteran craftspeople produce only about two inches of textile a day Traditional looms can be feet long have thousands of parts and require dozens of steps to operate by artisans who sing ballads to memorize the process From this creative matrix emerges luminous cloth with patterns woven from silk gold and peacock feather yarn The earliest examples of Chinese brocade are from the Tang dynasty A D Regional varieties developed across the country including in Nanjing and Chengdu home to silk museums where tourists can now buy authentic brocade scarves and bags Plan the ultimate Silk Road adventure through Central Asia The complicated fabric cannot be replicated by factories It can be woven only on the traditional loom says Feng Zhao honorary director of the China National Silk Museum in Hangzhou This authenticity appeals to older Chinese people who appreciate brocade from their hearts Zhao says and view it as a proud symbol of cultural heritage An inch of brocade such as this Han dynasty armband was once believed to be worth an ounce of gold Photograph by ROBERT CLARK Nat Geo Image Collection And increasingly up and coming Chinese fashion creators are collaborating with traditional weavers to emblazon garments with symbols such as phoenixes clouds and dragons Designer Chen Liwen last year launched a range of scarves and accessories targeted at Gen Z consumers that feature the hu bu tiger pattern Ancient brocade then seems tightly threaded into China s future Ronan O Connell is an Australian journalist and photographer who shuttles between
